# Methodology (Translated to Markdown)

## 0) About this methodology — who we are studying and why

This methodology provides a structured approach to researching and designing for two distinct target cohorts that our health app serves. The DO/SAY/VALUE framework is not just a theoretical model; it is applied directly to these real-world populations to understand their unique needs, behaviors, and decision-making processes.

### Target Cohorts

**1. Patients with Unknown or Unexplained Symptoms**
- **Definition:** Individuals experiencing persistent, often debilitating health issues who have yet to receive a definitive diagnosis.
- **Goal:** We want to understand their "diagnostic odyssey"—the journey they take through the medical system, the tools they use to track and communicate their symptoms, and the emotional and social hurdles they face while seeking legitimacy for their condition.

**2. Health Insurers (Payers)**
- **Definition:** Organizations and decision-makers responsible for evaluating, procuring, and integrating digital health solutions for their members.
- **Goal:** We want to understand their procurement workflows, evaluation criteria for new vendors, and the specific clinical and financial proof points they require before authorizing a pilot or a full-scale rollout.

By applying this methodology to these cohorts, we ensure that our product decisions are grounded in the actual behaviors (DO), stated needs (SAY), and underlying priorities (VALUE) of those who will use and pay for the app.

## 1) What do customers say? Interview planning and preparation is fundamental

### Identify customer targets
- End consumers
- Direct customers
- Decision makers
- Influencers
- By geography
- By age
- By gender
- By other segmentation criteria

### Identify customers to interview
- Our customers
- Competitors' customers
- Dual customers
- High-volume vs low-volume customers
- Customers using competing technologies

### Prepare interviews

#### Unmet and unarticulated needs
1. How is the customer currently solving the problem? What product are they using, and why?
2. What unsolved problem exists? Are there opportunities for a new solution or a new way of doing things?
3. If the customer had a choice, which product or brand would they buy now, and why? Which would they not buy, and why?

#### Choice criteria and competitive ratings
4. What are the customer's choice criteria used to make the purchase decision? What is the weight of each criterion?
5. How do current products and competitors rate, and why?
6. What does the customer specifically like or dislike about competitors' products? What problems arise when using them?
7. Which specific features, attributes, and performance characteristics are must-haves versus desirable but non-essential?
8. What trade-offs would the customer make?

#### Value proposition
9. How does the customer use the product within their system?
10. What is the customer's broader economic view?

## 2) Kawakita principles allow a better understanding of consumer needs

### Traditional market research vs Kawakita principles

| Traditional market research | Kawakita principles |
|---|---|
| **Focus:** Start with hypotheses and test them against reality. | **360-degree view:** Start without hypotheses; observe reality from multiple angles to discover something new. |
| **Rigid schedule:** Fixed hours for focus groups. | **Stepping-stones:** Keep a flexible schedule and move to the next person/place as opportunities arise. |
| **Structured predetermined plan:** Follow a fixed research plan. | **By chance:** Leverage unexpected opportunities; increase sensitivity to detect issues previously unnoticed. |
| **Objective processes:** For example, statistical summaries. | **Intuitive capability:** If intuition signals relevance, investigate, even if logic suggests low importance. |
| **Quantitative data** | **Qualitative data:** Cases and personal experience can matter more than raw numbers. |

## 5) Six actions critical to managing customer-experience journeys

Source: *From touchpoints to journeys*, McKinsey, March 2016.

1. Step back and identify the nature of customer journeys from the customer's point of view.
2. Understand how stakeholders navigate touchpoints throughout the journey.
3. Anticipate stakeholder needs, expectations, and desires during each stage.
4. Build understanding of what is working and what is not.
5. Set priorities for the most important gaps and opportunities to improve the journey.
6. Address root causes and redesign journeys for a better end-to-end experience.

## 6) A Practical Guide to Creating Synthetic Personas

Transform research insights into actionable user archetypes that drive better product and service decisions. This framework helps designers, researchers, and strategists build evidence-informed personas that teams actually use.

### Step 1: Define the decision your persona must support

#### Start with purpose
Before creating any persona, define the decision it must inform. Personas without a clear purpose become shelf-ware.

Common decision types:
- Product or feature prioritization
- Brand positioning and messaging strategy
- Journey redesign and optimization
- Market segmentation hypothesis testing
- Channel strategy and media planning
- Service experience innovation

Deliverable:
- Write 1-2 sentences: `We are creating personas to help us decide ___ for ___ audience in ___ context.`

### Step 2: Set boundaries and ethics

Synthetic personas can become biased if protected traits are used as causal explanations. Use behavioral and situational variables as primary drivers.

Guidelines:
- Focus on behavior: needs, constraints, triggers, risk tolerance, literacy levels, and routines
- Use minimal demographics: include demographic information only when it changes a design requirement
- Create guardrails: define 3-5 rules to prevent stereotype logic during drafting

### Step 3: Choose your persona architecture

Select the architecture that best fits your strategic decision:
- Role-based: buyer, user, and influencer perspectives when purchase and usage are separated
- Need-state-based: jobs-to-be-done and desired outcomes when motivation drives the experience
- Behavior-based: usage frequency, channel preference, or decision style when actions matter most
- Attitude-based: mindsets such as innovation adoption or trust orientation when psychology shapes choices

Rule of thumb:
- Start with 3-5 personas; more often signals segmentation needs rather than persona clarity.

### Step 4: Gather inputs (real or proxy evidence)

Even synthetic personas should be evidence-informed. Model observed patterns; do not invent users.

Minimum evidence sources:
- Qualitative: interviews, open-ended survey responses, customer reviews, call center logs, social listening themes
- Quantitative: CRM patterns, web analytics, purchase frequency, churn/retention data, NPS verbatims
- Context: category norms, competitor reviews, industry reports

If no data is available, run a starter evidence sprint:
- Analyze 10-20 reviews per brand (yours and competitors)
- Conduct 5 short interviews (15 minutes each) or one open-ended micro-survey
- Pull basic funnel metrics showing top drop-off points

Deliverable:
- One-page insight inventory organized by recurring cross-source patterns.

### Steps 5-6: Extract variables, then cluster into archetypes

Before writing persona narratives, convert patterns into variables to avoid fictional storytelling.

1. Build a variable bank:
   - jobs to be done, pain points, desired outcomes, barriers, triggers, decision criteria, channel preferences, objections
2. Create a variables table:
   - rows = variables, second column = supporting evidence notes
3. Identify separating variables:
   - pick 2-3 variables where users differ most (for example: risk tolerance, time sensitivity, trust needs)
4. Map and cluster patterns:
   - plot on a simple 2x2 or 3x2 grid, then select 3-5 distinct clusters

Deliverables:
- Variable bank table (Step 5)
- Persona list with working titles (Step 6), for example: `Time-Starved Optimizer`, `Trust-First Skeptic`

### Steps 7-8: Write structured models and design implications

#### Persona structure (Step 7)
Keep each persona to one page max:
- Name and archetype label
- Primary job-to-be-done
- Context of use (where/when/why)
- Top 3 motivations and pain points
- Ranked decision criteria
- Barriers, anxieties, and triggers
- Preferred channels and touchpoints
- Quote that captures the mindset

Avoid biographical fluff; focus on decision-relevant details.

#### Design implications (Step 8)
Translate traits into action:
- Messaging: what claims resonate, what to avoid
- Offer: what bundle, price, or guarantee reduces friction
- UX/Service: what steps must be simplified
- Trust: what proof points are required
- Channel: where to reach them and in what format

Target output:
- 6-10 actionable bullets per persona.

### Steps 9-10: Validate and operationalize your personas

#### Reality check
Validate with:
- Sales teams tagging cases
- Customer interviews
- CRM mapping
- A/B tests of persona-aligned messages

#### Make them usable
Operationalize by:
- Embedding personas in briefs
- Tying personas to KPIs
- Using personas in journey mapping
- Creating do/don't checklists for messaging and UX decisions

Personas fail when they remain posters instead of workflow tools.

Deliverables:
- Validation log documenting what held up and what changed (Step 9)
- One-page `How to use these personas` guide for the team (Step 10)

Note:
- Validation does not need to be expensive, but it must exist to prevent drift into fiction.

### Step 11: Establish a refresh cycle

Treat personas as living documents.

1. Quarterly review:
   - light check to confirm personas still map to observed behavior and current priorities
2. Annual refresh:
   - full review with new research, updated market conditions, and evolving needs
3. Version control:
   - document changes and rationale (`v1.1`, `v1.2`), and retire personas that no longer match reality

Deliverable:
- Persona changelog tracking what changed, why, and when.

### Common failure modes to avoid

- Demographics in disguise: shallow profiles without behavioral depth or decision criteria
- No observable behavior: models that cannot be validated against real actions
- Missing decision hierarchy: preferences listed without ranking what matters most under trade-offs
- No barriers or triggers: no friction or activation moments, so no behavior-change insight
- Too many personas: more than 5 usually indicates segmentation confusion

Remember:
- Great personas are decision tools, not creative-writing exercises.
- Keep them focused, evidence-based, and operationally useful.

## 7) Applying DO/SAY/VALUE to our target cohorts

This section instantiates the abstract DO/SAY/VALUE framework by mapping it specifically to our two primary research cohorts. By grounding these principles in real-world behaviors and stated needs, we ensure our research translates into actionable product requirements that solve actual problems for both patients and payers.

### 7.1) Cohort 1: Patients with Unknown or Unexplained Symptoms

Patients in this group are navigating what is clinically known as a "diagnostic odyssey"—a prolonged period of uncertainty where their symptoms are not yet matched to a known condition. Their behavior is driven by a deep need for clinical answers and social legitimacy.

#### **DO: Behavioral Reality (Implicit Observation)**
We focus on what patients actually do to manage their health and navigate the system, rather than just what they claim:
- **Diagnostic Crowdsourcing:** Patients frequently turn to Reddit (e.g., r/diagnoseme, r/chronicillness) or TikTok to crowdsource opinions from peers. This is an active search for patterns that doctors may have missed.
- **Advanced Symptom Logging:** They use tracking tools like Bearable, Unhide, or custom Excel spreadsheets to capture minute-by-minute fluctuations in symptoms, triggers (food, sleep, stress), and medication efficacy.
- **Medical Navigation & Doctor Shopping:** They visit multiple specialists—often jumping between neurologists, rheumatologists, and endocrinologists—in search of a provider who will take their case seriously.
- **Self-Advocacy Workflows:** They often arrive at appointments with printed "medical binders" or organized digital folders containing their entire history, assuming (correctly) that the provider hasn't read their chart.
- **Peer Search Behaviors:** They use hashtags and specific keywords to find others with "orphan" symptoms, forming informal support networks for "invisible illnesses."

#### **SAY: Stated and Emerging Needs (Explicit Communication)**
We listen for the emotional themes and specific linguistic markers that reveal their underlying mindset:
- **Medical Gaslighting:** A dominant theme is the feeling of being dismissed by providers. They say: "They told me it was just anxiety," or "I feel like I'm being gaslit every time I enter a clinic."
- **The Need for a Name:** They explicitly state: "I just need a name for this." For these patients, a diagnosis is not just about treatment—it’s about qualifying for disability, explaining their condition to an employer, or gaining social legitimacy.
- **Frustration with Fragmentation:** They complain about repeating their story: "Why do I have to tell the same story to every single doctor? Doesn't anyone talk to each other?"
- **The "Invisible" Struggle:** They use phrases like "none of us are lying" or "I look healthy on the outside, but I'm falling apart on the inside."

#### **VALUE: Prioritization (Kano Classification)**
We categorize potential features based on their impact on patient satisfaction and retention:
- **Must-Haves (Basic Expectations):** Physician-ready clinical data exports. This means generating a 1-page summary (PDF/CSV) that fits into a standard 15-minute clinical encounter. Without this, the app is just a diary.
- **Performance Attributes (Competitive):** Advanced symptom pattern visualization. Patients value the ability to see longitudinal trends and correlations (e.g., "my joint pain peaks 48 hours after a high-stress event").
- **Surprises and Delighters (Exciting Quality):**
  - **AI-Based Rare Disease Fact Sheets:** Providing patients with high-quality, peer-reviewed information on potential rare conditions that match their data.
  - **Community Matching:** Automatically connecting them with other patients who share highly specific, non-obvious symptom clusters.

#### **Key Patient Segmentations:**
- **The Empowered Researcher:** High health literacy, tech-savvy, and actively pushes doctors for specific tests. They see the app as a weapon in their advocacy.
- **The Overwhelmed Seeker:** Low health literacy, limited support system, and feeling defeated by the system. They need the app to act as a guide and a cognitive aid.

### 7.2) Cohort 2: Health Insurers (Payers)

Insurers evaluate digital health solutions through the lens of risk mitigation, Medical Loss Ratio (MLR) improvement, and operational efficiency. They are wary of "point solution fatigue" and demand high levels of evidence.

#### **DO: Behavioral Reality (Implicit Observation)**
We map the institutional behaviors and procurement cycles of insurance decision-makers:
- **Rigorous Procurement Workflows:** They run multi-month (often 6-18 months) procurement cycles that involve legal, security, and clinical risk assessments.
- **Adherence to Evidence Frameworks:** They use standardized rubrics like the PHTI's "Evidence DEFINED" framework to judge whether a solution provides actual clinical value.
- **API and Integration Mandates:** They mandate FHIR (Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources) and HL7 standards. If a solution doesn't plug into their existing data lake, it is a non-starter.
- **Vendor Risk Management:** They spend significant time auditing a vendor's SOC2 Type II, HIPAA compliance, and data privacy policies before any clinical discussion occurs.

#### **SAY: Stated and Emerging Needs (Explicit Communication)**
We listen for the objections and ROI-driven language used in boardrooms and procurement meetings:
- **Point Solution Fatigue:** "We have 14,000 digital health ventures knocking on our door. We cannot manage another siloed app."
- **Hard ROI Focus:** "Engagement metrics like 'daily active users' are nice, but show me the hard ROI. I need to see MLR (Medical Loss Ratio) or COR (Cost of Revenue) data."
- **Non-Negotiable Validation:** "Clinical validation is our first gate. If you don't have peer-reviewed data or a proven clinical outcome from a previous pilot, we can't move forward."
- **Integration Concerns:** "We don't want another login. It has to be zero-lift for our members and our staff."

#### **VALUE: Prioritization (Kano Classification)**
We prioritize enterprise features based on the insurer's decision-making hierarchy:
- **Must-Haves (Basic Expectations):** Full SOC2, HIPAA, and FHIR compliance. These are "table stakes" for entering the insurance market.
- **Performance Attributes (Competitive):** Verified ROI and medical savings data. Insurers value a vendor who can prove they reduce hospital readmissions or ER visits for high-risk patients.
- **Surprises and Delighters (Exciting Quality):**
  - **Zero-Lift Connectors:** Native integrations for Epic, Cerner, or Salesforce Health Cloud that allow their staff to see app data without leaving their primary interface.
  - **Member NPS Lift:** Demonstrable improvement in member satisfaction scores that help the insurer with retention and Star Ratings.

#### **Key Insurer Segmentations by Role:**
- **The Analyst:** Primarily concerned with data accuracy, auditability, and technical specifications. They want to know "how it works" under the hood.
- **The Product Owner:** Focused on speed to pilot and member user experience. They want to know "how it feels" for the member.
- **The Ops Manager:** Focused on avoiding new manual workflows. They ask: "Will this create more work for my care managers?"
- **The Executive:** Focused on macro-level ROI, strategic alignment, and the solution's impact on the overall cost of care across the member population.
